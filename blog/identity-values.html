Identity and Values

A quote that best summarises my core values is from Robin Williams in a 60 Minutes tribute to his friend and mentor, Jonathan Winters. When asked what he had learned from Jonathan, he responded that “The world is open for play, that everything and everybody is mockable, in a wonderful way.” In a video essay, Evan Puschak explores this, saying “…it suggests rightly, that humanity and our shared reality is made up of fluid elements of language and social structures which are sometimes codified into rules and pressures and laws, but that even the most rigid feature of what man makes can be turned flexible again and malleable again and funny, with play.”

These first 6 words, “The world is open for play” have always stuck with me and carry multiple meanings. One is that we should always test and question the things around us. To look at life from different angles and be open to reevaluating our understandings. Another is a reminder that we exist in this space and time and we have this life to work with, that we can act upon the world and make of it what we will. It’s about approaching the beautiful absurdity of the world and all the forces acting upon it with a certain lightness. That we can forge our own paths outside of convention; that we don’t have to slot into place.

More succinctly, I define my core values as curiosity, empathy, and creativity. When asked, people closest to me reenforced these as being my values. (!) Thirst for knowledge.

A time that challenged my core values was when I worked in retail for one of the telecommunication companies. There were two young customers who were interested in signing onto phone plans as they came with free handsets. I was worried that the plans were larger than what they needed and they would end up paying much more over the life of the contract. Also, I was concerned they weren’t taking the contract seriously. Ideally, I would have spoken more candidly about my concerns but I felt caught by my responsibilities to my employer, as well as whether it was appropriate to question their request. In hindsight, I wish I had done more to make sure they were making a good decision. Although I was empathetic to their situation, I didn’t act on it and although I had a responsibility to my employer, I didn’t push back against the situation.

I don’t know how my culture has influenced my values. I’m mindful that I’m privileged to have grown up in a middle-class family and that my value system might be a product of freedom that accompanies that. I would say that my personality type as described by the Myers–Briggs Type Indicator has a greater influence. When I first tested as INTP and read through the profile it was uncanny how it aligned with my experiences. While I remain skeptical of the value of the system, I do feel it provides some insight or at least a bedrock for introspection.

The strengths of the INTP, which are listed as being analytical, original, open-minded, curious, objective, all ring true and are compatible/overlap with my stated values. They are also good qualities in a web developer, partially being analytical and curious.

The weakness are listed as disconnected, insensitive, impatient, dissatisfied, perfectionistic. Dissatisfaction and perfectionism often trip me up, locking me in moments of decision paralysis and procrastination.
